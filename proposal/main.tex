\section{Chapter 1: Logical Foundation}

\subsection{Problem Statement}

E-commerce businesses should be able to accurately forecast individual customers'
purchasing intentions in real time, enabling personalised marketing, dynamic inventory
management, and reduced cart-abandonment rates. Ideally, such systems would exploit
both \emph{transactional history} (what a customer bought) and \emph{clickstream behaviour}
(what a customer browsed, dwell time, and navigation path) to build rich, contextually
aware models.

Most operational e-commerce recommendation engines rely on collaborative filtering or
simple rule-based heuristics that consider only past purchases and ignore rich behavioural
signals embedded in clickstream logs. Studies such as \cite{kumar2022clickstream} and
\cite{zhang2023deep} confirm that combining these two data modalities can lift prediction
accuracy, yet most real-world systems have yet to integrate them. Moreover, existing
approaches often suffer from data sparsity, cold-start problems for new users, and poor
generalisation across product categories.

Without accurate purchase-intent prediction, retailers lose significant revenue through
missed up-sell opportunities, excessive promotional spending on unlikely buyers, and
high cart-abandonment rates. The inability to leverage clickstream data also means that
short-term behavioural shifts (e.g.\ browsing a competitor's product before purchasing)
remain invisible to current models.

\subsection{Research Objectives}

\textbf{General Objective:}
To design and implement a hybrid data mining system that integrates transaction and
clickstream data for accurately predicting customer purchasing patterns in e-commerce
environments.

\textbf{Specific Objectives:}
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=1.5cm]
  \item \textbf{Data Integration Pipeline:} By the end of Week~6, construct and
        validate a preprocessing pipeline that merges transaction records and clickstream
        logs from the REES46 e-commerce dataset, achieving less than 5\% missing-value rate
        after cleaning.

  \item \textbf{Predictive Model Development:} By the end of Week~10, train and tune
        at least three classification models (XGBoost, Random Forest, and LSTM-based
        sequential model) on the integrated dataset, targeting a minimum F1-score of 0.80
        on the held-out test set.

  \item \textbf{Comparative Evaluation and Reporting:} By the end of Week~13, produce
        a rigorous comparative evaluation of all models using Accuracy, Precision, Recall,
        F1-Score, AUC-ROC, and Mean Reciprocal Rank, and document findings in a
        reproducible GitHub repository with full experimental logs.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Research Gap Analysis (Synthesis)}

Recent literature demonstrates substantial progress in applying machine learning and
sequential deep learning techniques to e-commerce behavioural modelling. Sakar et al.\
\cite{sakar2019realtime} established an important benchmark by showing that LSTM-style
sequence learning can outperform conventional classifiers such as random forests and
support vector machines when predicting purchasing intention from online shopping
sessions. Their work confirms the value of sequential modelling for understanding
customer browsing transitions. Van den Broeck \cite{broeck2020sequential} further
supports the use of ordered behavioural sequences by showing that recurrent approaches
are better suited to shopping-intention prediction than static feature-only baselines.

More recent studies expanded this direction through feature aggregation and tree-based
learning. Liu et al.\ \cite{liu2023feature} demonstrate that carefully aggregated
clickstream features improve purchase-intent prediction, while Wang et al.\
\cite{wang2025sales} show that boosting-oriented models remain effective on structured
transaction data. Kumar and Singh \cite{kumar2026clickstream} reinforce the relevance of
sequential learning by showing that clickstream-based deep models capture complex
behavioural dependencies that simpler methods often miss.

Collectively, these studies establish a strong state-of-the-art baseline for e-commerce
purchase prediction using either transaction-focused modelling, clickstream-based
sequential learning, or feature aggregation. However, the reviewed studies largely
evaluate these methods in isolation. Existing research gives limited attention to the
integration of transactional purchase history with sequential clickstream behaviour
within a unified comparative framework that also prioritises interpretability for
small-to-mid-sized retailers.

This research addresses that gap by developing a unified predictive framework that
combines transaction-derived features and clickstream behavioural sequences from the
REES46 dataset while comparatively evaluating XGBoost, Random Forest, and LSTM
models. In addition, the study incorporates SHAP-based explainability mechanisms to
improve transparency and practical usability for retailers seeking interpretable customer
purchase predictions.

\section{Chapter 3: Technical Methodology \& Experimental Design}

\subsection{System Architecture Overview}

The system follows a four-layer pipeline architecture:

\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=1.4cm, itemsep=2pt]
  \item \textbf{Data Ingestion Layer:} Raw CSV files from the REES46 dataset
        (events.csv --- clickstream; purchases.csv --- transactions) are ingested into a
        Pandas DataFrame. A user--session key is used to join both sources.

  \item \textbf{Pre-processing \& Feature Engineering Layer:} Cleaning, encoding, and
        feature extraction (see Section 3.3). Output is a unified feature matrix $X \in
        \mathbb{R}^{n \times d}$ with binary purchase label $y \in \{0,1\}$.

  \item \textbf{Modelling Layer:} Three model families are trained and tuned via
        5-fold stratified cross-validation: (a) XGBoost classifier, (b) Random Forest,
        and (c) a two-layer LSTM with attention for sequential clickstream input.

  \item \textbf{Evaluation \& Explainability Layer:} Metrics are computed on a
        held-out 20\% test split. SHAP TreeExplainer is applied to the best-performing
        tree model to generate global and local feature importance explanations.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Tool Justification}

\begin{description}[leftmargin=1.4cm, style=nextline, itemsep=3pt]
  \item[\textbf{Python 3.11 + scikit-learn 1.4:}]
    Industry-standard ML ecosystem with native support for cross-validation, pipeline
    construction, and metric computation. Chosen over R for stronger community support
    for deep learning integration.

  \item[\textbf{XGBoost 2.0 vs. LightGBM:}]
    XGBoost is retained because recent transaction-oriented and clickstream-based studies
    show that boosting-style tree models remain competitive on structured behavioural data
    and are especially useful when features are heterogeneous, sparse, and partly
    aggregated. The literature base supports using a strong boosting benchmark alongside
    sequential neural models.

  \item[\textbf{PyTorch 2.2 (LSTM) vs. TensorFlow/Keras:}]
    PyTorch was selected to implement the LSTM architecture because the reviewed
    sequential-intent studies show that recurrent models are effective for modelling
    ordered clickstream behaviour and variable-length session traces. PyTorch's dynamic
    computation graph and \texttt{PackedSequence} API allow efficient processing of
    variable-length sessions while supporting rapid experimentation.

  \item[\textbf{SHAP 0.44:}]
    Provides model-agnostic and tree-specific explainability, directly addressing the
    interpretability limitations identified across the reviewed literature.

  \item[\textbf{Pandas + Matplotlib + Seaborn:}]
    Used for data wrangling, exploratory data analysis, and result visualisation.
    Preferred over external dashboarding tools for full reproducibility in code.
\end{description}

\subsection{Data Strategy}

\textbf{Dataset:} REES46 E-Commerce Behaviour Data, available on
\href{https://www.kaggle.com/datasets/mkechinov/ecommerce-behavior-data-from-multi-category-store}{Kaggle}
(IEEE DataPort mirror). Contains approximately 67 million events (views, cart additions,
purchases) and 9.7 million unique users across October--November 2019.

\textbf{Pre-processing Steps:}
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=1.4cm, itemsep=2pt]
  \item \textbf{Deduplication:} Remove duplicate event rows using
        (\texttt{user\_session}, \texttt{event\_time}, \texttt{product\_id}) composite key.
  \item \textbf{Missing Value Handling:} Drop rows where \texttt{user\_id} is null ($< 1\%$);
        impute missing \texttt{category\_code} with ``unknown'' category.
  \item \textbf{Session Segmentation:} Define a session as a contiguous sequence of events
        with inter-event gap $\leq 30$ minutes; assign unique \texttt{session\_id}.
  \item \textbf{Feature Extraction:}
        \begin{itemize}[itemsep=1pt]
          \item \emph{Transactional:} purchase frequency, recency (days since last purchase),
                average order value, product category diversity score.
          \item \emph{Clickstream:} session length (events), view-to-cart ratio,
                average dwell time proxy (event gap), number of distinct products viewed,
                cart-abandonment flag.
        \end{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Label Construction:} Binary label $y=1$ if a purchase event occurs within
        the same session as the feature observation window, else $y=0$.
  \item \textbf{Class Imbalance:} Apply SMOTE oversampling on training fold only to address
        the $\approx$1:9 purchase-to-non-purchase imbalance.
  \item \textbf{Normalisation:} StandardScaler applied to continuous features for tree
        models; MinMaxScaler used for LSTM input sequences.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Evaluation Metrics}

Let TP, FP, TN, FN denote true positives, false positives, true negatives, and false negatives respectively.

\[
\text{Accuracy} = \frac{TP + TN}{TP + TN + FP + FN}
\qquad
\text{Precision} = \frac{TP}{TP + FP}
\qquad
\text{Recall} = \frac{TP}{TP + FN}
\]
\[
F_1 = 2 \cdot \frac{\text{Precision} \times \text{Recall}}{\text{Precision} + \text{Recall}}
\qquad
\text{AUC-ROC} = \int_0^1 \text{TPR}(t)\,\mathrm{d}\,\text{FPR}(t)
\]
\[
\text{MRR} = \frac{1}{|Q|}\sum_{i=1}^{|Q|} \frac{1}{\text{rank}_i}
\]

Primary success criterion: $F_1 \geq 0.80$ and $\text{AUC-ROC} \geq 0.88$ on the held-out test set for the best-performing model.

\subsection{Ethical Considerations}

\textbf{Data Privacy (GDPR Compliance).}
The REES46 dataset is fully anonymised: user identifiers are hashed and no personally
identifiable information (PII) such as names, addresses, or payment details is present.
Because the data is publicly released on Kaggle under a permissive licence, its use in
academic research complies with GDPR Article~89 (research exemptions). No additional
consent or data processing agreement is required. Should the methodology be adapted to
real production data, a Data Protection Impact Assessment (DPIA) would be mandatory
under GDPR Article~35.

\textbf{Bias and Fairness in the AI Model.}
Purchase prediction models can inadvertently encode demographic or socioeconomic bias
if historical purchasing patterns reflect structural inequalities (e.g.\ certain user
segments having fewer purchase events due to economic constraints rather than low
intent). To mitigate this, the study will (i) inspect feature distributions across
proxy demographic segments where inferrable from behavioural signals, (ii) report
model performance broken down by user activity quartile, and (iii) avoid including
features that directly proxy for protected characteristics.

\textbf{Environmental Impact.}
Training tree ensembles and a moderate-scale LSTM on approximately 2 million sampled
records (post-stratification) on a single GPU is estimated to consume fewer than 5~kWh
total energy across all experimental runs---negligible compared to large-scale
foundation-model training. All experiments will be logged via CodeCarbon to transparently
report carbon emissions.
